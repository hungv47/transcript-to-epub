# On Finishing

**00:00:00**: Most of the work I'm proud of, I almost didn't finish. Not because it was hard to make, but because it was hard to stop making it. There's a moment near the end of every project where the thing is basically done, and that's exactly when it gets dangerous.

**00:00:18**: Because near the end, you finally understand what you were trying to do. And understanding it makes you want to start over and do it properly this time. That feeling is real, and it's a trap. The version in your head is always better than the version on the table, because the version in your head doesn't have to exist yet.

**00:00:42**: I used to think finishing was about discipline — about forcing yourself across some line. I don't think that anymore. I think finishing is a skill, like any other, and you get better at it by doing it badly a few times.

**00:00:58**: Here's the practice that helped me. Decide what "done" means before you start, and write it down somewhere you can't quietly edit later. Not "done is when it's great." Done is when it does the three things you said it would do. When it does them, you ship it, even if you've thought of a fourth thing.

**00:01:20**: The fourth thing is the next project. That's the reframe. You're not abandoning the good idea — you're saving it for a thing that hasn't been ruined by a deadline yet. Finishing one piece is how you earn the right to begin the next one.

**00:01:38**: And something happens when you ship that doesn't happen any other way. The work meets the world. People use it, or ignore it, or argue with it, and all of that teaches you things that no amount of polishing in private ever could. A finished, flawed thing in someone's hands beats a perfect thing in your drawer. Every time.

**00:02:00**: So my advice, if you want it, is small. Lower the stakes of finishing. Make it a habit instead of an event. Ship the thing that does the three things. Then go for a walk, and let the fourth thing find you.
