# Notes from a Long Walk

**00:00:00**: >> Devi: My guest today walks. A lot. He's written about thinking on foot, and I wanted to start with the obvious question — why walking, specifically?
**00:00:12**: >> Tomas: Because it's the one kind of motion that leaves the mind free. You're moving fast enough that your body is occupied, and slow enough that nothing demands your attention. The world goes by at the speed of a sentence.
**00:00:28**: >> Devi: The speed of a sentence. I like that. So you're not walking to clear your head, you're walking to use it?
**00:00:36**: >> Tomas: Both, really. The first ten minutes are for clearing. You're carrying the day with you, all the small noise. Then somewhere around the second mile, the noise runs out, and what's left is the actual question you've been avoiding.
**00:00:53**: >> Devi: And do the answers come?
**00:00:56**: >> Tomas: Not answers. Better questions. That's the thing people get wrong. A walk doesn't hand you a conclusion. It rearranges what you already know until the shape of the problem is visible. The conclusion is your job, later, at the desk.
**00:01:14**: >> Devi: So the desk and the walk are doing different work.
**00:01:18**: >> Tomas: Completely different. The desk is for deciding. The walk is for noticing. If you try to decide while you walk, you stop walking — you stand still in the middle of the path arguing with yourself. And if you try to notice while you sit at the desk, you just check your email.
**00:01:38**: >> Devi: That's painfully accurate. What about ideas — where do they actually come from, in your experience?
**00:01:45**: >> Tomas: From collisions. You carry two unrelated things around long enough, and on some ordinary walk they bump into each other. You didn't make the idea. You just kept both things alive long enough for them to meet.
**00:02:02**: >> Devi: So the work is keeping things alive.
**00:02:05**: >> Tomas: The work is keeping things alive, and then showing up to walk. That's the whole method. It's embarrassingly simple, which is probably why so few people do it.
**00:02:16**: >> Devi: Tomas, thank you. I'm going to go for a walk.
**00:02:19**: >> Tomas: That's the right response to everything, honestly.
